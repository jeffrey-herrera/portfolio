The future of creative work depends on judgment, not output.

I've been watching how people in creative fields are adapting to AI tools, and I notice two very different responses. One group is asking: how do I use this to make more? The other is asking: how do I use this to make better decisions?

The second group is more interesting.

Output is the part AI is genuinely good at. Volume, variation, speed — those problems are largely solved. What it can't do is tell you which direction is right. Which version of an idea is worth pursuing. When something is technically correct but aesthetically wrong. When the brief itself needs to change.

That's judgment. And judgment is built from experience, context, and values — none of which AI inherits from you automatically.

The designers who are thriving with these tools aren't the ones treating them like faster production pipelines. They're the ones who've gotten very clear about what their judgment is actually worth — and who are using AI to handle everything that doesn't require it.

The question isn't whether AI will replace creative work. The question is whether you're investing in the kind of creative thinking that can't be replicated. That's taste, strategic vision, deep knowledge of a particular domain, and the ability to make calls when there's no clear answer.

Those things become more valuable, not less, in a world where output is cheap.
