<mark>Every few months someone shares a new AI-generated image and the comments split the same way. Half the room thinks it's impressive. The other half thinks it's slop. Both are responding to the output. Neither is talking about what actually makes creative work hard.</mark>

It was never the making. It was always the deciding.

What fits the intent. What earns its place. What looks right but isn't quite there. Those calls don't get easier when generation speeds up. If anything, they get harder. More options, more moments where someone needs to say: not that one.

That's the part that doesn't compress. You can generate a hundred directions in the time it used to take to sketch three. The speed is real. But someone still has to know which one is worth pursuing, and why, and what to do when the answer isn't obvious. When output is cheap, the quality of decisions becomes the differentiator.

Creative leadership has always been about making judgment visible. Building the criteria that help teams make better decisions without needing a sign-off on every one. AI doesn't change that responsibility. It just raises the stakes for it.

Where I think this goes next: agents that learn your taste. Not tools that generate on command, but systems you can actually teach. Ones that absorb your criteria, your patterns, the calls you keep making, and start handling the smaller decisions so your judgment can go toward the ones that actually matter. The creative director who figures out how to teach their taste to a system will have a real edge. Not because they're using better tools. Because they've done the harder work of understanding their own judgment well enough to articulate it.

<mark>That's not a distant future. It's already being built.</mark>
