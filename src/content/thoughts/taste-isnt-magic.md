<mark>Taste gets talked about like it's something you're born with. An eye for quality. A gut feeling. Magic.</mark>

It's not. What we call taste is pattern recognition. Built up through exposure, repetition, and context. From seeing enough work to know what tends to hold together, what usually falls apart, and what fits a specific moment versus just resembling it.

That's why it feels intuitive to the person using it and opaque to everyone else. The thinking is compressed. Years of reference collapse into a fast read on something, and from the outside it just looks like instinct.

It's also why taste is hard to teach. We explain outcomes instead of the patterns behind them. This works, that doesn't...without saying what we're actually responding to.

Good creative judgment isn't about stronger opinions. It's about recognizing familiar patterns under new constraints. Knowing when something aligns with the intent versus when it only looks like it does on the surface.

Treated as magic, taste becomes something only one person can exercise. Calls get made without criteria. Feedback lands as preference instead of judgment. Over time, that creates dependency rather than capability.

Taste doesn't disappear when you explain it. It becomes something a team can actually use.
