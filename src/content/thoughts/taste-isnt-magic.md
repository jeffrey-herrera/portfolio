<mark>You've worked with that person. The one who walks into a review, glances at three directions, and knows immediately which one is right.</mark>

That's what most people mean when they talk about taste. The person with the eye. The one who just knows. It gets treated like magic, something you're born with or you're not.

But when you look at what's actually happening, taste isn't mysterious. It's pattern recognition built up over time. From seeing enough work to know what tends to hold together, what usually falls apart, and what fits a specific moment versus just resembling it. The thinking is compressed. Years of exposure collapse into a fast read, and from the outside it just looks like instinct.

That's why taste is hard to teach. We explain outcomes instead of the patterns behind them. This works, that doesn't, without ever saying what we're actually responding to.

Treated as magic, taste becomes something only one person can exercise. Feedback turns into "I'll know it when I see it." Decisions lose their criteria. Over time that creates dependency, not capability. The whole team waiting on one person to tell them if something is right.

Good creative judgment isn't about stronger opinions. It's about recognizing familiar patterns under new constraints. Knowing when something actually aligns with the intent versus when it only looks like it does on the surface. That's a skill. It can be named, shared, and built on.

<mark>Taste doesn't disappear when you explain it. It becomes something a team can actually use.</mark>
